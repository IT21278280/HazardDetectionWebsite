Introduction

Deaf drivers face significant risks in Colombo's traffic (85 dB noise), where auditory cues like horns are critical. Initiated in July 2024 by Group 24-25J-132 at SLIIT, this project employs AI-driven horn detection (94.2% accuracy), lipreading (10.8% WER), and behavior monitoring (96% accuracy) using IoT devices. It aims to reduce response times by 1.2 seconds through visual and tactile alerts, tested in 50 real-world trials.

Our Domain

The literature review explores existing assistive technologies for deaf drivers, highlighting critical limitations in current solutions. Early systems such as Beritelli and Casale’s siren detection (88% accuracy) and DriveAlert’s mirror-mounted lights lacked directional awareness and performed poorly in noisy urban environments. Vibrotactile cushions provided generalized vibrations but could not differentiate sound sources. Horn detection studies, like Zhao’s TDOA-based localization, performed well in labs but failed under real-world urban noise (85 dB), suffering from high false positives and latency. Lipreading models like LipNet and adaptations by Alamri et al. showed promise but struggled with lighting variations and driver movements in cars. Most behavior monitoring systems—such as those by Alamri and Kang—relied on auditory alerts, making them inaccessible for deaf users. None of the reviewed systems integrated all critical components (horn detection, lipreading, behavior monitoring) into a unified solution tailored for deaf drivers. The review identifies a gap for a noise-robust, direction-aware, real-time assistive system with non-auditory feedback. The proposed system, combining CNNs, TDOA, IoT, and haptic feedback, achieves superior performance—94.2% horn detection accuracy, 10.8% WER in lipreading, and 96% behavior detection accuracy—positioning it as a significant advancement in inclusive road safety technology.

The research identifies a significant gap in existing assistive technologies for deaf drivers—specifically, the absence of a unified, context-aware, and non-auditory hazard detection and communication system suitable for real-world urban conditions. Current solutions lack integration across key areas: horn detection systems offer either detection or directionality, but not both, and struggle with false positives in high-noise environments like Colombo. Lipreading technologies have yet to be adapted for vehicular contexts, facing challenges with occlusions, lighting variability, and driver head movement. Behavior monitoring systems either rely on auditory feedback or require costly, high-performance GPUs, making them impractical for large-scale use in developing countries. Furthermore, no existing solution addresses emergency communication needs during accidents for deaf drivers. The literature emphasizes the demand for directional alerts and accessible feedback—both of which are insufficiently addressed in present offerings. This project fills these voids by integrating advanced CNNs, TDOA localization, LipCam-based lipreading, and sensor-fusion-based behavior monitoring into a low-latency, IoT-driven solution. The resulting system delivers precise, real-time alerts through visual and haptic channels, tailored for deaf drivers navigating complex urban traffic, with performance validated under realistic field conditions in Colombo. This represents a novel contribution to the accessibility and automotive safety domains.
