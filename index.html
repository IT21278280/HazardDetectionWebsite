Autonomous IoT-Enabled Hazard Detection System for Deaf Drivers

Developed by Group 24-25J-132 at SLIIT, this project enhances safety for deaf drivers in Colombo's noisy urban environment (85 dB average). It leverages IoT and AI, achieving 94.2% horn detection accuracy, 10.8% Word Error Rate (WER) in lipreading, and 96% accuracy in behavior monitoring, reducing response time by 1.2 seconds. Launched in July 2024, it includes real-time alerts via visual and tactile feedback.

Introduction

Deaf drivers face significant risks in Colombo's traffic (85 dB noise), where auditory cues like horns are critical. Initiated in July 2024 by Group 24-25J-132 at SLIIT, this project employs AI-driven horn detection (94.2% accuracy), lipreading (10.8% WER), and behavior monitoring (96% accuracy) using IoT devices. It aims to reduce response times by 1.2 seconds through visual and tactile alerts, tested in 50 real-world trials.

Our Domain

Literature Survey

Existing systems like Beritelli [1] (2015) achieve 85% horn detection but lack directional awareness. Zhao [3] (2018) reports 12% WER in lipreading, yet integration with IoT for deaf drivers remains unaddressed. Our survey of 20 papers highlights a gap in real-time, multi-modal hazard detection.

Research Gap

No system integrates horn detection, lipreading, and behavior monitoring for deaf drivers. Current solutions (e.g., Patel [4], 2020) focus on hearing aids (90% efficacy) but neglect visual/tactile feedback, leaving a 30% response delay in noisy environments like Colombo.

Problem Statement

Deaf drivers miss auditory warnings (e.g., horns at 85 dB) in Colombo traffic, leading to a 15% higher accident rate (SLIIT Traffic Study, 2023). Existing tech fails to provide real-time, direction-aware alerts, with latency exceeding 300 ms.

Objectives

Achieve >90% horn detection accuracy, <12% WER in lipreading, and <200 ms latency. Enhance safety with 95% driver response rate in 100 simulated scenarios, validated by May 2025 testing phases.

Methodology

Uses Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for horn detection (trained on 1,000 samples), Time Difference of Arrival (TDOA) for directionality, and IoT with ESP32 and Raspberry Pi. Lipreading employs Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) with 500-hour dataset, tested in 50 urban trials.

Technology

Leverages TensorFlow for AI models, Flutter for UI, MQTT for IoT communication, and NVIDIA Jetson Nano for processing. Hardware includes ESP32 (sensors), Raspberry Pi (control), and LED/vibration modules, deployed in 10 prototype vehicles.
