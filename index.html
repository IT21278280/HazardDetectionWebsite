Autonomous IoT-Enabled Hazard Detection System for Deaf Drivers

Developed by Group 24-25J-132 at SLIIT, this project enhances safety for deaf drivers in Colombo's noisy urban environment (85 dB average). It leverages IoT and AI, achieving 94.2% horn detection accuracy, 10.8% Word Error Rate (WER) in lipreading, and 96% accuracy in behavior monitoring, reducing response time by 1.2 seconds. Launched in July 2024, it includes real-time alerts via visual and tactile feedback.

Introduction

Deaf drivers face significant risks in Colombo's traffic (85 dB noise), where auditory cues like horns are critical. Initiated in July 2024 by Group 24-25J-132 at SLIIT, this project employs AI-driven horn detection (94.2% accuracy), lipreading (10.8% WER), and behavior monitoring (96% accuracy) using IoT devices. It aims to reduce response times by 1.2 seconds through visual and tactile alerts, tested in 50 real-world trials.

Our Domain

The literature review explores existing assistive technologies for deaf drivers, highlighting critical limitations in current solutions. Early systems such as Beritelli and Casale’s siren detection (88% accuracy) and DriveAlert’s mirror-mounted lights lacked directional awareness and performed poorly in noisy urban environments. Vibrotactile cushions provided generalized vibrations but could not differentiate sound sources. Horn detection studies, like Zhao’s TDOA-based localization, performed well in labs but failed under real-world urban noise (85 dB), suffering from high false positives and latency. Lipreading models like LipNet and adaptations by Alamri et al. showed promise but struggled with lighting variations and driver movements in cars. Most behavior monitoring systems—such as those by Alamri and Kang—relied on auditory alerts, making them inaccessible for deaf users. None of the reviewed systems integrated all critical components (horn detection, lipreading, behavior monitoring) into a unified solution tailored for deaf drivers. The review identifies a gap for a noise-robust, direction-aware, real-time assistive system with non-auditory feedback. The proposed system, combining CNNs, TDOA, IoT, and haptic feedback, achieves superior performance—94.2% horn detection accuracy, 10.8% WER in lipreading, and 96% behavior detection accuracy—positioning it as a significant advancement in inclusive road safety technology.

The research identifies a significant gap in existing assistive technologies for deaf drivers—specifically, the absence of a unified, context-aware, and non-auditory hazard detection and communication system suitable for real-world urban conditions. Current solutions lack integration across key areas: horn detection systems offer either detection or directionality, but not both, and struggle with false positives in high-noise environments like Colombo. Lipreading technologies have yet to be adapted for vehicular contexts, facing challenges with occlusions, lighting variability, and driver head movement. Behavior monitoring systems either rely on auditory feedback or require costly, high-performance GPUs, making them impractical for large-scale use in developing countries. Furthermore, no existing solution addresses emergency communication needs during accidents for deaf drivers. The literature emphasizes the demand for directional alerts and accessible feedback—both of which are insufficiently addressed in present offerings. This project fills these voids by integrating advanced CNNs, TDOA localization, LipCam-based lipreading, and sensor-fusion-based behavior monitoring into a low-latency, IoT-driven solution. The resulting system delivers precise, real-time alerts through visual and haptic channels, tailored for deaf drivers navigating complex urban traffic, with performance validated under realistic field conditions in Colombo. This represents a novel contribution to the accessibility and automotive safety domains.

Deaf drivers face critical road safety challenges due to their inability to perceive auditory cues like vehicle horns and emergency sirens, which are essential for quick decision-making in chaotic urban traffic environments. In Colombo, where traffic noise levels reach 85 dB and visual stimuli are overwhelming, these drivers are at elevated risk of collisions and delayed responses. Conventional Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) primarily rely on auditory alerts, rendering them ineffective for the deaf. Existing assistive devices, such as LED-based warnings or vibrational cues, provide minimal context, lack directional awareness, and perform poorly in noise-heavy environments. Furthermore, deaf drivers face communication barriers during emergencies, unable to convey urgent information like “I need medical help” to responders. This communication gap increases their vulnerability in post-accident scenarios. The overarching problem is the lack of an integrated, robust, and real-time system that can detect hazards, enable emergency communication, and monitor driver attentiveness—all without relying on hearing. Thus, there is an urgent need for a scalable, affordable, and IoT-enabled solution that uses non-auditory feedback mechanisms to support deaf drivers. This system must perform reliably in complex, high-noise environments, reduce response time, and provide accessible communication and behavioral alerts to empower safer independent mobility.

The project’s primary objective is to enhance road safety and communication capabilities for deaf drivers by developing an IoT-enabled, real-time system that integrates hazard detection, lipreading, and driver behavior monitoring, all delivered through accessible non-auditory feedback. To achieve this, six specific objectives were set. First, the system must detect and localize vehicle horns with over 90% accuracy and within ±5° directional precision in noisy environments (85 dB). Second, it should support emergency communication through a lipreading module that transcribes spoken phrases from dashboard camera footage with less than 12% Word Error Rate (WER), even under lighting variations between 50–500 lux. Third, the driver behavior monitoring component must detect distractions like texting or lane drifting with over 95% accuracy using visual and sensor data. Fourth, all subsystems must operate with a total latency under 200 ms to ensure timely responses at typical urban speeds (50 km/h). Fifth, field validation must confirm usability and robustness through 50 real-world trials with deaf drivers, targeting an average user rating of 4.7/5. Finally, the system should be cost-effective (~22,000 LKR/unit) and scalable for mass production, utilizing open-source tools and affordable local hardware to reach Sri Lanka’s 75,000 deaf drivers and potentially expand regionally.

The system architecture is composed of three main components integrated within an IoT framework: horn detection and localization, lipreading for emergency communication, and driver behavior monitoring. Horn detection uses MEMS microphones mounted at the rear of the vehicle to capture sound, processed through a CNN for classification and Time Difference of Arrival (TDOA) for direction estimation, with alerts transmitted via MQTT to a mobile app and Bluetooth-connected haptic wristband. The LipCam component uses a dashboard-mounted 1080p camera, capturing mouth movements, preprocessed using Dlib facial landmarks and augmented for lighting variability. A 3D CNN and GRU-based architecture, trained on the GRID corpus, performs real-time transcription with <100 ms latency. For behavior monitoring, dual cameras and MPU-6050 sensors capture driver and vehicle dynamics. A MobileNetV2 CNN model classifies visual behaviors, while an LSTM processes temporal sensor data. Outputs are fused to detect distractions, triggering visual alerts in the app and haptic feedback on the wristband. All modules process data locally on ESP32, Raspberry Pi, or Jetson Nano devices, ensuring privacy and minimizing latency. The system was tested in both lab and field settings with 12 deaf drivers in Colombo, validating performance under real-world conditions including high noise, low light, and traffic complexity.

The proposed system leverages a combination of cutting-edge technologies to deliver a real-time, inclusive driving aid for deaf drivers. Core machine learning techniques include Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for horn and siren detection, and a hybrid 3D CNN-Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) model for lipreading. The driver behavior monitoring system employs MobileNetV2 for vision-based classification and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks for temporal pattern recognition using sensor data. IoT is central to the system, with edge devices like ESP32 handling horn detection and Bluetooth communication, Raspberry Pi 4 managing sensor fusion and siren detection, and NVIDIA Jetson Nano powering the resource-intensive LipCam module. Real-time communication is facilitated through the MQTT protocol, ensuring low-latency data transfer to the mobile app and haptic wristband. Audio preprocessing is done using Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs), and all data is processed locally to comply with GDPR and Sri Lanka’s privacy laws. The mobile app, developed in Flutter, delivers intuitive visual cues, while haptic feedback is conveyed via Wear OS smartwatches or wristbands. All components are designed to be power-efficient and suitable for deployment in typical vehicle environments, including variable lighting and high-noise urban settings. Open-source tools such as TensorFlow and Librosa further enhance the system’s scalability and cost-effectiveness.
